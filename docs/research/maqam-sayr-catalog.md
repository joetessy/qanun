# Per-Maqam Sayr Catalog — *Inside Arabic Music* (Farraj & Abu Shumays, OUP 2019), Chapter 24: Maqam Index

**Page mapping confirmed:** book page P = PDF page P+35 (offset +35). Chapter 24 runs printed pp. 371–417 (PDF 406–452). Source PDF: `/Users/yusuf/Downloads/dokumen.pub_inside-arabic-music-arabic-maqam-performance-and-theory-in-the-20th-century-0190658363-9780190658366.pdf`

**How to read the sayr-diagram notation (Box 24.1, p.378):** Each jins is a rectangle labeled with its name + the scale degree of *its tonic* relative to the maqam tonic (e.g. "Nahawand 5" = Jins Nahawand whose tonic is the 5th degree). For ajnas whose real tonic sits below the bottom of their basic scale, two numbers are given: the degree of the first note of the fragment / the degree of the actual tonic (e.g. "Upper Rast 5/8" = Upper Rast starting on degree 5, tonic on degree 8). "LO" = lower octave. **Vertical position** roughly reflects frequency (most common ajnas near the *bottom*/closest to the start rectangle; rarer ones near the top). **Rounded corners** = starting jins; **thick border / white-on-black** = ending jins (qafla); both styles combined = start-and-end. Lines = bidirectional modulation paths; a line ending in an arrow = one-directional only.

Note on note-names: the book is largely degree-relative and does not pin every maqam to an absolute tonic in the index. I give the conventional tonic where the book states it (Rast = C, Kirdan = C5, Yakah = G3, Rahat al-Arwah = Bb, etc.); otherwise tonic is given by degree.

## RAST FAMILY (printed pp. 377–385)

**Family overview:** Bound by Jins Rast on the tonic (a half-flat-3rd jins, tonic conventionally C). Rast is the most important/prominent maqam of the tarab repertoire ("If your night will be long, sing in Rast"). Because of its popularity, by tradition the "main maqam" Rast absorbs *all* the branch members' ajnas as available modulation options — so the family is unusually interconnected. Shared ghammaz is the 5th degree (G), with Jins Nahawand on 5 as a universal return-transition device back to the root Jins Rast. Two governing sayr archetypes: an **ascending** Suznak-type sayr (root jins → Hijaz/other ajnas emphasizing the ghammaz on 5 → briefly the octave → descend back to root), which became dominant mid-20th c.; and an older **descending-from-the-octave** sayr (Kirdan/Sazkar/Dalanshin/Mahur type) that fell out of fashion.

### Maqam Rast (family: Rast; printed pp.377–379)
- **Tonic:** C (Jins Rast on tonic).
- **Scale (fig 24.1):** Rast 1 (C) + Upper Rast 5/8 + Nahawand 5 — i.e. Jins Rast on tonic, with Nahawand and Upper Rast structures above the ghammaz.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.2), lower→upper, ordered bottom-up by frequency:** Rast 1 (start, on tonic C); Nikriz 1; Sikah 3 / Mustaʿar 3 (low secondary ajnas on the 3rd); Sikah Baladi 8 / Saba Dalanshin 6/8; Nahawand 8 / Jiharkah 8 (octave-region ajnas); Rast 8 (octave); Bayati 5 / Saba 5 (on the ghammaz); Upper Rast 5/8 / Nahawand 5 / Hijaz 5 (the principal ghammaz cluster on degree 5); plus Upper Rast 5/8 LO (lower octave).
- **Ghammaz / dominant:** G (5th degree) — the most active modulation region (Nahawand 5, Hijaz 5, Bayati 5, Saba 5).
- **Notes of melodic emphasis:** tonic C; ghammaz G; octave C (Rast 8).
- **SAYR:** Starts on the root Jins Rast (C). Develops upward emphasizing the ghammaz on 5 with a cluster of ajnas there (Nahawand 5, Hijaz 5, Bayati/Saba 5); reaches the octave region (Rast 8, Nahawand 8, Jiharkah 8); cadences back down to the root Jins Rast. (Older alternative: full descent from the octave, the Kirdan pathway.)
- **Typical modulations (ordered, most idiomatic first):** to Nahawand on 5 (universal transition back to root); to Hijaz on 5 (this *is* Maqam Suznak — practically obligatory in Rast taqasim/mawwal); to Bayati on 5 (this is the Nairuz pathway — "expect to hear this modulation frequently"); to Saba on 5; to Upper Rast / octave (Rast 8); to Sikah/Mustaʿar on 3; to Nikriz on 1; to Saba Dalanshin on the octave (the Dalanshin color).
- **Siblings:** see below — Suznak (=Rast+Hijaz on 5), Nairuz/Yakah (=Rast+Bayati on 5), Kirdan/Sazkar (Rast from the octave down), Dalanshin (octave descent starting with Saba Dalanshin), Suzdalara, Mahur. They share the Rast scale but differ in sayr/emphasis.

### Maqam Kirdan / Maqam Sazkar (family: Rast; printed p.380)
- **Tonic:** effectively C; "Kirdan" is the Arabic 24-tone name for C5 (the octave above the Rast C4). This maqam is a version of Rast whose **sayr starts on the octave tonic (C5) and descends to the root tonic**.
- **Scale (fig 24.3):** Upper Rast 5/8 + Rast 1 + Sazkar 1.
- **Sazkar distinction:** nearly identical to Kirdan but with prominence of the **raised 2nd scale degree** (D#) in some melodic passages (not universal). *sazkar* = Persian *saz* (instrument) + *kar* (work).
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.4), bottom-up:** Rast 1 / Sazkar 1 / Sikah 3 (start-and-end cluster at root); Rast 8; Hijaz 5; Nahawand 5; Upper Rast 5/8.
- **Ghammaz:** 5 (G).
- **SAYR:** Begins at the octave (Upper Rast 5/8 region), works **downward** through Nahawand 5 / Hijaz 5 to the root Jins Rast / Sikah 3, cadencing on the tonic.
- **Performance note:** The authors argue its practical existence as an independent maqam is arguable — except in a few cases it has become "more of a pathway within the larger Maqam Rast sayr." Songs: muwashshahat "ya shadi al-alhan," "sihtu wajdan"; Leila Mourad's "hayrana leh."

### Maqam Suznak (family: Rast; printed p.381)
- **Tonic:** C (Rast 1).
- **Scale (fig 24.5):** Rast 1 + Hijaz 5 — Jins Rast on tonic, **Jins Hijaz on the 5th (ghammaz)**. This is the defining feature.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.6), bottom-up:** Rast 1 (start, on tonic); Sikah 3; Nahawand 5; Rast 8; Saba 5 / Bayati 5; Nahawand 8; Hijaz 5 (end/qafla region, on ghammaz).
- **Ghammaz:** 5 (G) — strongly emphasized via Jins Hijaz.
- **SAYR (most common sayr of the whole Rast family from mid-20th c. on):** Start with the root Jins Rast → **ascend to Hijaz on 5** and other modulations emphasizing the ghammaz on 5 → briefly reach the octave tonic → **descend back to the root**. Cadence on tonic Rast.
- **Modulations (ordered):** to Hijaz on 5 (definitional, near-obligatory); to Bayati/Saba on 5; to Nahawand on 5; to the octave (Rast 8 / Nahawand 8); to Sikah on 3.
- **Sibling note:** "Suznak is the most popular version of Maqam Rast used in the 20th century" — i.e. it largely *is* how Rast is performed; it superseded the descending Kirdan sayr. *suznak* = "burning, ardent" (Persian). Songs: Umm Kulthum's "ghanni li shwayya shwayya"; "malik ya hilwa."

### Maqam Nairuz / Maqam Yakah (family: Rast; printed pp.381–382)
- **Tonic:** Nairuz on C; **Yakah** is the archaic version based on G3 (the note "Yakah" in the Arabic superscale) rather than C4. *nairuz* = Persian *nowruz*, "new day."
- **Scale (fig 24.7):** Rast 1 + Bayati 5 — Jins Rast on tonic with **Jins Bayati on the 5th**.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.8), bottom-up:** Rast 1 (start, tonic); Nahawand 5 / Hijaz 5 (lower in stack); Nahawand 8; Saba 5; Bayati 5 (start-and-end region — the defining jins on the ghammaz).
- **Ghammaz:** 5 (G), carrying Jins Bayati.
- **SAYR:** Root Jins Rast → up to Bayati on the ghammaz (5), with Saba 5 / Hijaz 5 / Nahawand 5 also available → octave (Nahawand 8) → return to Rast.
- **Status / note:** "quite rare as its own independent entity," but prominent **as a sayr within Suznak/Rast** because of the affinity between Jins Bayati on 5 and Jins Hijaz. The dawr "asl il-gharam" and the taqtuqa "ghanni li shwayya shwayya" make very prominent use of the Nairuz pathway (Bayati on 5) — when drawn out, a true manifestation of Maqam Nairuz. The muwashshah "ya hilalan" is the best-known Nairuz example (but Min Kunuzina files it under Rast). "Listeners should expect to hear this modulation frequently in performances of Rast."

### Maqam Dalanshin (family: Rast; printed pp.382–383)
- **Tonic:** C (descent type, like Kirdan/Sazkar).
- **Scale (fig 24.9):** Saba Dalanshin 6/8 + Upper Rast 5/8 + Rast 1 — i.e. a striking **Saba on the octave** (flat-9th / dark Saba color above) descending into Rast.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.10), bottom-up:** Rast 1 / Sazkar 1 (start-and-end at root); Rast 8 / Jiharkah 8; Nahawand 5; Upper Rast 5/8; Saba Dalanshin 6/8 (top).
- **SAYR:** Very close to Kirdan/Sazkar (descent from the octave above Rast) **but begins with the very striking Saba Dalanshin on the octave** — flat 9th degree, dark Saba color — transitioning down through Upper Rast / Nahawand 5 to the root Rast. The "jarring dissonance" of this sayr makes it very recognizable.
- **Note:** rare and archaic now, but much more popular early 20th c. Its sayr is prominent in the middle of the qadd "ya mal ish-sham" and muwashshah "mala il-kasat." Abdel Wahab's mawwal "kul illi habb itnasaf" extends Dalanshin, even reaching a "shocking and unique" modulation to ʿAjam (via the once-common use of Jins Saba Dalanshin within Maqam ʿAjam ʿUshayran). (Jins Saba Dalanshin was formerly "Secondary Saba.")

### Maqam Suzdalara (family: Rast; printed pp.383–384)
- **Tonic:** C (Rast 1).
- **Scale (fig 24.11):** Nahawand 5 + Rast 1 — Jins Rast on tonic, Jins Nahawand on 5. *suzdalara* = "lover's fire" (Persian).
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.12) — very small:** Rast 1 (start); Sikah 3; Mustaʿar 3; Nahawand 5. That's the whole diagram.
- **SAYR:** Minimal. Jins Nahawand on 5 of Rast (the universal transition device back to root Jins Rast) is the whole point — it signals the return to root from other modulations.
- **Status:** "almost nonexistent as an independent maqam," but its sayr is *obligatory in nearly every performance/song in every branch of Maqam Rast*. The one well-known song is "yana yana" by Sabah (pop-redone by Rola Saad), a true Suzdalara example by virtue of *never using* Jins Upper Rast (or even Jins Hijaz).

### Maqam Mahur (family: Rast; printed pp.384–385)
- **Tonic:** C, version "from the octave."
- **Scale (fig 24.13):** Upper ʿAjam 5/8 + Rast 1 — the **Egyptian** Mahur uses **Jins Upper ʿAjam above Jins Rast, rather than Jins Upper Rast**. *mahur* = "new moon"/"crescent" (Persian).
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.14) — small:** Rast 1 (start, tonic); Hijaz 5; Nahawand 5; Upper ʿAjam 5/8 (top).
- **SAYR:** Like Kirdan, basically a version of Rast starting at the octave; descends, but with the ʿAjam-colored upper region.
- **Note:** Whether Mahur exists as a distinct Arabic maqam is debatable; it exists in Ottoman Turkish (where Rast's 3rd/7th are intoned much higher, ~major scale). The Egyptian hybrid (Upper ʿAjam over Rast) sounds so different from other Rast-family modulations that "it is almost never included as a modulation in tarab performances of Rast." Songs: muwashshahat "bi sifatin jaʿalatni," "hayyara al-afkar"; Samaʿi Mahur by Inʿam Labib.

## BAYATI FAMILY (printed pp.385–387)

**Family overview:** Built on Jins Bayati on the tonic (tonic conventionally D). One of the most popular maqamat in Arabic music; less prominent than Rast in tarab but "far outstrips Rast in the folk and folk-inspired repertoire," and the most common maqam in the region as a whole (Lebanese, Turkish, Kurdish, Iraqi, Iranian repertoires). Few branch maqamat, but a large sayr from its popularity. The 5th degree functions as a note of tension resolving down to 4 (the Husayni emphasis), and the modulation to **Hijaz on 4** is almost obligatory in Bayati. Scale (fig 24.15): Bayati 1 + Nahawand 4 + Rast 4 (i.e. Bayati on tonic; the 4th degree carries both Nahawand and Rast structures).

### Maqam Bayati (family: Bayati; printed pp.385–386)
- **Tonic:** D (Bayati 1).
- **Scale (fig 24.15):** Bayati 1 + Nahawand 4 + Rast 4.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.16), bottom-up by frequency:** Bayati 1 (start, tonic); Hijaz 1 / Saba 1 (low, below tonic); Rast 4 LO; ʿAjam 3 / Jiharkah 3; Hijaz 4 (very prominent — see below); Jiharkah 7 / Rast 7; Bayati 8 (octave); ʿAjam 6 / Upper ʿAjam 3/6; Husayni 5/4; Nahawand 4 / Rast 4 (the principal 4th-degree cluster).
- **Ghammaz / emphasis:** the 4th degree (G) is the chief modulation hub (Nahawand 4, Rast 4, Hijaz 4); the **5th (A) is a tension tone resolving down to 4** (Husayni behavior).
- **SAYR:** Start on root Jins Bayati (D). Develop primarily around the 4th degree — Nahawand 4, Rast 4, and especially **Hijaz on 4** (almost obligatory, even if brief); the Husayni emphasis touches the 5th as tension → resolves to 4; reaches the octave (Bayati 8) and upper-region ajnas (ʿAjam 6, Rast/Jiharkah 7). Cadence to the root.
- **Modulations (ordered):** to Hijaz on 4 (near-obligatory); to Nahawand on 4; to Rast on 4; Husayni emphasis on 5→4; to the octave Bayati 8; to ʿAjam on 6 / Upper ʿAjam; to ʿAjam/Jiharkah on 3; to Hijaz/Saba on 1 (below the tonic).
- **Siblings:** Bayati Shuri (lowered 5th, Hijaz-4 emphasis), Husayni (5→4 emphasis, descends from 5).

### Maqam Bayati Shuri (family: Bayati; printed pp.386–387)
- **Tonic:** D (Bayati 1).
- **Scale (fig 24.17):** Bayati 1 + Hijaz 4 — a version of Bayati with a **lowered 5th scale degree**; named for Persian dastgah *Shur*. (Footnote: the 5th is variable, not always lowered in Dastgah Shur.)
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.18), bottom-up:** Bayati 1 (start, tonic); Bayati/Saba 4; Rast 7 / Nahawand 7 / Bayati 8; Hijaz 4 (start-and-end, on the 4th).
- **SAYR:** In the sharqi tradition full songs in Bayati Shuri aren't very common, but the **modulation to Hijaz on 4 is almost obligatory (even if brief)** in Bayati songs/improvisations — that Hijaz-4 emphasis with the lowered 5th *is* Bayati Shuri.
- **Note:** Early Umm Kulthum taqtuqa "mihtar ya nas" uses the archaic sayr with Nahawand 7 (breaking octave equivalence). Examples: muwashshahat "hibbi daʿani lil-wisal," "talaffata al-dhabiyu"; dawr "dayyaʿati mustaʿbal hayati"; the qadd "ah ya hilu" (ends in Bayati Shuri, opens in ʿAjam 6).

### Maqam Husayni (family: Bayati; printed p.387)
- **Tonic:** D (Bayati 1); sayr **emphasizing the 5th degree (A)**.
- **Scale (fig 24.19):** "Husayni 5→4" + Bayati 1 — the dashed slur indicates the 5th resolving down to the 4th.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.20), bottom-up:** Bayati 1 (start, tonic); ʿAjam 6; Nahawand 4; Rast 4; Husayni 5/4 (start-and-end region — the defining emphasis).
- **SAYR:** Distinguished by **emphasizing the 5th degree as a note of tension resolving down to 4** — i.e. it tends to live high and **descend from 5**. This emphasis is obligatory *within* Maqam Bayati (e.g. in the verse of Farid al-Atrash's "nura nura," and the 2nd khana of Samaʿi Bayati of Ibrahim al-ʿAryan).
- **Note:** not very common as an independent maqam in Arabic tradition, "but in the folk music of the wider region, this is actually the most common variant of Bayati." Examples: muwashshahat "hal ʿala l-astar," "khala al-ʿidhar," "billadhi askara."

## SIKAH FAMILY (printed pp.388–393)

**Family overview:** The tonic of the Sikah family is a **half-flat note** (Sikah, conventionally E half-flat), giving these maqamat "an unusual feeling of tonicization and resolution." The most prominent member is actually **Huzam** (not Sikah itself), and **Jins Hijaz on the 3rd scale degree is the default for the whole family**. Central to tarab. Many theorists mis-classify the family as having "Rast on the 3rd"; the authors stress Sikah actually tonicizes Rast on the **6th** degree (the notes leading up form Upper Rast from the 3rd). Note unusual octave non-equivalence in some members (e.g. Bastanikar's Saba on 3 eliminates the octave).

### Maqam Huzam / Maqam Rahat al-Arwah (family: Sikah; printed pp.388–389)
- **Tonic:** Sikah (E half-flat) — i.e. the family's half-flat tonic, with Jins Sikah on tonic (degree 1) and Jins Hijaz on degree 3. **Rahat al-Arwah** = a transposition of Huzam **down to Bb**; its sayr is now indistinguishable from Huzam but its mood earned a separate name ("comfort/rest of the souls").
- **Scale (fig 24.21):** Sikah 1 + Hijaz 3 + Rast 6 — Jins Sikah on tonic, **Hijaz on 3**, Rast on 6.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.22), bottom-up by frequency:** Sikah 1 (start, tonic); Rast 6 LO; Mukhalif Sh. 1 / Mustaʿar 1 (low secondary on tonic); Nahawand 3 / Upper Rast 3/6; Bayati 3; (emphasis on 5); Sikah 8 / Rast 6; Hijaz 3 (very common, on the 3rd).
- **Ghammaz / emphasis:** the **3rd degree** (Hijaz) and the **6th degree** (Rast tonicization, = Sikah 8 region); plus an "emphasis on 5."
- **SAYR:** Start on the root Jins Sikah (half-flat tonic). Default move is **Hijaz on the 3rd**; develop up through Bayati 3 / Nahawand 3 / Upper Rast 3/6 to **Rast on the 6th** (and Sikah 8 at the octave), with an emphasis on the 5th along the way. Cadence resolving to the half-flat Sikah tonic.
- **Modulations (ordered):** to Hijaz on 3 (family default, most common); to Rast on 6 / Upper Rast (octave tonicization); to Bayati on 3; to Nahawand on 3; emphasis on 5; to Sikah/Mustaʿar/Mukhalif on 1.
- **Siblings:** Sikah (rarer, tonicizes on 6); ʿIraq (descends, rare); Bastanikar (Saba on 3, no octave equiv.); Awj ʿIraq (octave-descent version of Huzam); Mustaʿar (Upper Rast 3/6 → Nahawand 3 emphasis). Examples: Umm Kulthum's adwar "emta-l-hawa," "inta fahim," taqtuqa "il-wardi gamil"; muwashshahat "ya ghusna naqa," "ayyuha al-saqi," "ya wahid al-ghid."

### Maqam Sikah (family: Sikah; printed p.389)
- **Tonic:** Sikah (E half-flat), but **tonicizes Rast on the 6th degree** (its defining trait), with the notes leading up forming Jins Upper Rast (from the 3rd to the 6th). Theorists confuse it by classifying it as "Rast on the 3rd."
- **Scale (fig 24.23):** Sikah 1 + Upper Rast 3/6 + Rast 6.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.24), bottom-up:** Sikah 3 (start; note the diagram labels the start "Sikah 3"); Rast 6 LO; Hijaz 3; (emphasis on 5); Nahawand 3 / Upper Rast 3/6; Sikah 8; Rast 6 (start-and-end, top — the 6th-degree Rast tonicization).
- **SAYR:** Starts on Sikah → ascends through Hijaz 3 / Nahawand 3 / Upper Rast 3/6 (emphasis on 5) → **tonicizes Rast on the 6th** (Rast 6 / Sikah 8) as the resolution region. This sayr appears prominently in the khana of muwashshah "jadaka al-ghaythu," and in verses 3–4 of Umm Kulthum's "il-wardi gamil" (though overall that song is Rahat al-Arwah).
- **Status:** a rarer member of the family.

### Maqam ʿIraq (family: Sikah; printed pp.389–390)
- **Tonic:** Sikah-region (the diagram's start jins is Sikah 1).
- **Scale (fig 24.25):** Sikah 1 + Bayati 3 + Rast 6 — Jins Sikah on tonic, **Bayati on 3**, Rast on 6.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.26), bottom-up:** Sikah 1 (start, tonic); Rast 6 LO; Hijaz 3 / Saba 3; Rast 6 / Sikah 8; Bayati 10; Nahawand 6; Bayati 3 (start-and-end, on the 3rd).
- **SAYR:** Begins on Sikah, with **Bayati on 3** as the characteristic jins (distinguishing it from Huzam's Hijaz-on-3); rises to Rast 6 / Sikah 8 and beyond (Bayati 10, Nahawand 6).
- **Status:** rare, archaic; prominent **as a sayr within Maqam Huzam** (analogous to Nairuz within Suznak), owing to the affinity between Jins Bayati and Jins Hijaz on the 3rd. Full-song recordings very hard to find. Examples: Samaʿi ʿIraq by Yusuf Basha; muwashshah "jalla man ansha jamalak."

### Maqam Bastanikar (family: Sikah; printed pp.390–391)
- **Tonic:** Sikah (E half-flat). *bastanikar* = Persian *basta nigar*, "bound to the beloved."
- **Scale (fig 24.27):** Sikah 1 + Saba 3 + Hijaz 5 (+ Nikriz 8) — **Saba on the 3rd**, which (because Saba lacks octave equivalence) *eliminates the octave above the Sikah tonic*; extreme example of non-equivalence at the octave.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.28), bottom-up:** Sikah 1 (start, tonic); Rast 6 LO; Hijaz 3 / Bayati 3; Rast 6 / Sikah 8; Hijaz 5; ʿAjam flat-8 / Nikriz flat-8 (flat-octave region, reflecting the lost octave equivalence); Saba 3 (start-and-end, on the 3rd — the defining dark jins).
- **SAYR:** Start on Sikah; the signature move is **Saba on the 3rd** (dark color), which dwells and creates anticipation before heading back to Sikah; the upper region reaches "flat-8" ajnas (ʿAjam flat-8, Nikriz flat-8) because the true octave is suppressed.
- **Status:** common in Ottoman Turkish, rare in Egyptian/Syrian, but popular as a modulation within Huzam improvisations; "dark and unusual," favored by instrumentalists showing off obscure modulations. Examples: Abdel Halim Hafez's "zalamu"; muwashshahat "aqbala al-subhu yughanni," "nuzhatu al-arwahi," "anshidi ya saba"; Umm Kulthum's "inti fakrani" (Bastanikar at "irhami albi") and "ahl il-hawa" (dwells on Saba at "we yisʾaluk ya leil…").

### Maqam Awj ʿIraq (family: Sikah; printed pp.391–392)
- **Tonic:** Sikah (E half-flat) — a version of Rahat al-Arwah/Huzam/ʿIraq whose **sayr starts at the octave tonic and descends to the root tonic**. "Awj" = the Bb octave note in the Arabic 24-tone scale (Persian/Arabic for "peak"/"highest point"); also the name of an Iraqi maqam whose sayr is very close — Awj ʿIraq is the Syrian translation of the Iraqi version.
- **Scale (fig 24.29):** Sikah 8 + Hijaz 3 + Sikah 1 (the Jins Sikah on the octave's bracket extends to include the two notes below its tonic, G and A-flat).
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.30), top-down (since it descends):** Sikah 8 (start, octave); Bayati 10; Rast 6; (emphasis on 5); Hijaz 3 / Bayati 3; Sikah 1 (end, root tonic).
- **SAYR:** Shares most of Huzam's ajnas but **starts high (octave Sikah) and descends** through Bayati 10 / Rast 6 / (emphasis on 5) / Hijaz 3 down to the root Sikah qafla.
- **Status:** like most octave-descending sayrs, archaic by mid-20th c., but still captured in Rahat al-Arwah improvisations. Min Kunuzina files muwashshahat "hayya wa nadim" and "jalla man ansha jamalak" here (though the latter's sayr matches ʿIraq); muwashshah "ya samiri daʿa sabri" (filed as Rahat al-Arwah) actually has the Awj ʿIraq sayr.

### Maqam Mustaʿar (also Mustaar) (family: Sikah; printed pp.392–393)
- **Tonic:** Sikah-region (Mustaʿar 1 = on the tonic). Defined by the **unique interval between the raised 2nd and the tonic**, giving a very distinctive, immediately recognizable sound.
- **Scale (fig 24.31):** Mustaʿar 1 + Nahawand 3 + Rast 6.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.32), bottom-up:** Mustaʿar 1 (start, tonic); Sikah 1; Hijaz 3; Upper Rast 3/6; (emphasis on 5); Nahawand 3 / Rast 6 (start-and-end region).
- **SAYR:** The signature transition is **Upper Rast 3/6 → Nahawand 3** (parallel to Rast's own Upper Rast 5/8 → Nahawand 5 on the way down to root) — making Mustaʿar a close cousin of Sikah; rarely heard without the Rast tonicization on 6 as well.
- **Status:** extremely rare as an independent maqam; more common as a jins or partial sayr within Huzam and Sikah — instantly identifiable when it appears as a secondary jins/sayr inside Huzam. No complete muwashshahat/qudud known; only complete Egyptian piece is the rare early "haramuha minni" (Fathiyya Ahmad). But the Mustaʿar sayr occurs in many songs (Umm Kulthum's "raqq il-habib," Leila Mourad's "aktiblak gawabat").

## HIJAZ FAMILY (printed pp.393–396)

**Family overview:** The iconic "Oriental" maqam for Western ears (distinctive augmented-2nd interval). Built on Jins Hijaz on the tonic (conventionally D). Named for the Hijaz region of Saudi Arabia; the iconic melodies are the *adhan*. Both an **ascending** and a **descending (from-the-octave)** sayr exist in the repertoire/improvisation but, unlike Rast vs Kirdan or Huzam vs Awj ʿIraq, they have *not* been given separate maqam names. Hijazkar (and transpositions) is the most common *variant*.

### Maqam Hijaz (family: Hijaz; printed pp.393–394)
- **Tonic:** D (Hijaz 1).
- **Scale (fig 24.33):** Hijaz 1 + Nahawand 4 + Rast 4 — Jins Hijaz on tonic; the 4th degree carries Nahawand and Rast.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.34), bottom-up:** Hijaz 1 (start, tonic); Rast 4 LO; Bayati 1 (on tonic); Nikriz 4; Hijazkar 8; Hijaz 8; Nahawand 4 / Rast 4 (the 4th-degree cluster; Rast 4 is itself a start-and-end-style rounded rectangle).
- **Ghammaz / emphasis:** the 4th degree (G) — Nahawand 4 / Rast 4 cluster.
- **SAYR:** Start on root Jins Hijaz; develop around the 4th (Rast 4, Nahawand 4, Nikriz 4); reach the octave (Hijaz 8 / Hijazkar 8); cadence to the tonic. (Ascending and descending-from-octave forms both exist, unnamed.)
- **Modulations (ordered):** to Rast on 4; to Nahawand on 4; to Nikriz on 4; to Hijazkar/Hijaz at the octave (8); to Bayati on 1; to Rast on 4 in the lower octave.
- **Siblings:** Hijazkar (Hijaz doubled — Hijaz on 1 and 5/octave; but really Nikriz-4→Hijaz-1 internally), Zanjaran (Hijaz on 1 + ʿAjam on 4). Examples: Umm Kulthum's "ana fi-ntizarak"; Farid al-Atrash's taqtuqa "hibbina"; Abdel Wahab's qasida "mudnaka"; Aleppan qudud "il-bulbul nagha," "qadduka al-mayyas," "heini w beinak," "ala al-ʿaqiq ijtamaʿna"; Aleppan Fasil "isqi al-ʿitash."

### Maqam Hijazkar (and its transpositions) (family: Hijaz; printed pp.394–395)
- **Tonic:** C (the scale was conceptually on C, the tonic of Hijaz when doubled). Named transpositions: **Shadd ʿAraban (low G)**, **Suzidil (low A)**, **Shahnaz (D)** — these carry meaning in the Turkish context but the authors found **no distinguishable sayrs** for the different Arabic transpositions. *kar* = "work" (Persian, referring to the doubling of Hijaz); *suzidil* = "burning heart"; *shahnaz* = *shah* (king) + *naz* (endearment).
- **Scale (fig 24.35):** Hijazkar 8 + Nikriz 4 + Hijaz 1 — appears to be two Hijaz ajnas stacked (on 1 and on 5), but in reality the primary ajnas in the sayr are **Nikriz on 4 descending to Hijaz on 1**, with melodies usually stopping on 5 (a suspended feeling of waiting to descend to 4).
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.36), bottom-up:** Hijaz 1 / Hijaz Murassaʿ 1 (start-and-end, on tonic); Husayni 4/5; Rast 4; Nahawand 4; Nikriz 4; Nahawand 8 ("archaic start"); Hijazkar 8 (top/octave).
- **SAYR:** Two primary ajnas — **Nikriz on 4 descending to Hijaz on 1**; melodies typically suspend on 5 (waiting to descend to 4). The archaic version shows striking lack of octave equivalence by using **Nahawand on the octave** (labeled "archaic start" — the descending-from-octave form).
- **Status:** the most common variant of Hijaz; even more common *as the principal modulation from Maqam Hijaz* — appearing as the khana/verse modulation in numerous muwashshahat and Hijaz songs. Examples: dawr "ya ma-nta wahishni"; 4th verse of Umm Kulthum's "ana fi-ntizarak"; Abdel Wahab's "khatwit habibi"; Farid al-Atrash's "raqsat al-jamal"; archaic-Hijazkar khana of "ma-htiyali" and "salla fina al-lahdhu hindiyya."

### Maqam Zanjaran (family: Hijaz; printed pp.395–396)
- **Tonic:** D (Hijaz 1).
- **Scale (fig 24.37):** ʿAjam 4 + Hijaz 1 — Jins Hijaz on tonic, **Jins ʿAjam on the 4th** (intervals match Rag Ahir Bhairav; semitonal, no quartertones).
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.38), bottom-up:** Hijaz 1 (start, tonic); Hijazkar 8; Nikriz 4; Nahawand 4; Saba Dalanshin 6/8; Hijaz 8; ʿAjam 4 (start-and-end, on the 4th — the defining jins).
- **SAYR:** Root Jins Hijaz → **ʿAjam on the 4th** as the characteristic resolution region, with Nikriz 4 / Nahawand 4 also available, up to the octave (Hijaz 8 / Hijazkar 8).
- **Status:** rare and beautiful, "more associated with Indian classical music." Rare as a modulation within Hijaz songs (because ʿAjam and Rast tend not to substitute for each other on the same tonic). Practically the only well-known song is the taqtuqa "ya halawit id-dunya" (Zakariyya Ahmad); several muwashshahat in Min Kunuzina (rarely performed); a striking mawwal by Abdel Wahab "amana ya leil" (rich Jins ʿAjam 4 development into a full ʿAjam presentation). Abu Shumays also composed a Dulab Zanjaran (2009 CD *Cinematic*).

## NAHAWAND FAMILY (printed pp.396–399)

**Family overview:** Basic scale = the Western minor scale; built on Jins Nahawand on the tonic (conventionally C). Main modulations mirror the natural/harmonic/melodic minor (Jins Kurd, Jins Hijaz, Jins Upper ʿAjam respectively on 5 — the last also called Maqam "Tarz Jadid"), but its sayr and melodic identity are quite distinct from Western music. The G transposition (Farahfaza) is its own maqam in Ottoman theory but doesn't really exist with a distinct sayr in Arabic. Important in tarab and mid-20th-c. dance/instrumental music; with the late-20th-c. decline of quartertonal maqamat, Nahawand (with Kurd) became one of the dominant maqamat in Arabic pop.

### Maqam Nahawand (family: Nahawand; printed pp.396–397)
- **Tonic:** C (Nahawand 1).
- **Scale (fig 24.39):** Nahawand 1 + Hijaz 5 + Kurd 5 — Jins Nahawand on tonic; the 5th degree carries Hijaz and Kurd structures.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.40), bottom-up:** Nahawand 1 (start, tonic); Nikriz 1; ʿAjam 3; Nahawand Murassaʿ 1; Nahawand 8 (octave; rounded/start-and-end); Upper ʿAjam 5/8; Bayati 5; Hijaz 5; Kurd 5 (the 5th-degree cluster).
- **Ghammaz:** the 5th degree (G) — Kurd 5 / Hijaz 5 / Bayati 5 / Upper ʿAjam 5/8 cluster (the three minor-scale flavors plus Bayati).
- **SAYR:** Start on root Jins Nahawand; develop around the ghammaz on 5 (Kurd 5 = natural minor, Hijaz 5 = harmonic, Upper ʿAjam 5/8 = melodic, plus Bayati 5); reach the octave (Nahawand 8); cadence to tonic.
- **Modulations (ordered):** to Kurd on 5; to Hijaz on 5; to Bayati on 5; to Upper ʿAjam on 5/8; to the octave Nahawand 8; to ʿAjam on 3; to Nikriz on 1; to Nahawand Murassaʿ on 1.
- **Siblings:** Nahawand Murassaʿ (lowered 5th — bluesy), ʿUshshaq Masri (Bayati on 5 emphasis). Examples: al-Qasabgi's "dhikrayati"; muwashshahat "munyati ʿazza-stibari," "ramani bi sahmi hawahu," "hat isqiniha," "lamma bada yatathanna," "subhana man sawwar husnak"; Aleppan qudud "sibuni ya nas," "il-fulli wil-yasamin," "il-ʿuzubiyya"; Umm Kulthum's "alf leila we leila"; Abdel Wahab's "balad il-mahbub"; "Longa Farahfaza" by Riyad al-Sunbati.

### Maqam Nahawand Murassaʿ (family: Nahawand; printed pp.397–398)
- **Tonic:** C (Nahawand Murassaʿ 1). *murassaʿ* = "inlaid/decorated" (Arabic).
- **Scale (fig 24.41):** Nahawand Murassaʿ 1 + Hijaz 4 — basically Nahawand with a **lowered 5th scale degree** (resembles American blues scales).
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.42), bottom-up:** Nahawand Murassaʿ 1 (start, tonic); Nahawand 1; Upper ʿAjam 5/8 LO; Hijaz 4; Bayati 5; Kurd 5; Upper ʿAjam 5/8; Nahawand Murassaʿ 8 (octave).
- **SAYR:** Like Nahawand but with the lowered 5th; develops to **Hijaz on 4** and the 5th-degree ajnas (Bayati 5, Kurd 5, Upper ʿAjam 5/8), to the octave.
- **Status:** most often a variant/ornament within Nahawand songs; occasionally prominent as a distinct maqam. Examples: Abdel Halim Hafez's "khusara"; verses of Horeyya Hassan's "min hubbi fik ya gari"; a dulab by Abu Shumays (2009 CD *Cinematic*).

### Maqam ʿUshshaq Masri (family: Nahawand; printed pp.398–399)
- **Tonic:** C (Nahawand 1).
- **Scale (fig 24.43):** Nahawand 1 + Bayati 5 — Jins Nahawand on tonic with **Jins Bayati on the 5th** (the distinguishing feature vs ordinary Nahawand's Hijaz/Kurd on 5).
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.44), bottom-up:** Nahawand 1 (start, tonic); ʿAjam 3; Rast 8; Nahawand 8; ʿAjam 10; Bayati 12; Bayati 5 (start-and-end, on the 5th — the defining jins).
- **SAYR:** Root Jins Nahawand → **Bayati on the 5th** as the characteristic region (instead of Hijaz/Kurd), reaching up to Rast 8 / Nahawand 8 and high ajnas (ʿAjam 10, Bayati 12).
- **Status:** hard to pinpoint full songs clearly in ʿUshshaq Masri vs Nahawand songs merely using Bayati on 5 (a prominent, important modulation within Nahawand). Seems to have been more common in early-20th-c. Nahawand (e.g. Abu al-ʿIla Muhammad: "layali Nahawand," "baʿd il-khisam"). Abdel Wahab's dawr "ahibb ashufak" is one piece that clearly articulates ʿUshshaq Masri.

## NIKRIZ FAMILY (printed pp.399–401)

**Family overview:** Built on Jins Nikriz on the tonic (conventionally C). Much less represented in the Arab tradition than in Ottoman Turkish / Eastern European / Roma music. Confusable with Hijaz (augmented 2nd between degrees 3 and 4), but melodies and sayr are completely distinct. The archaic Nikriz lacked octave equivalence — the ghammaz above ajnas on its ghammaz reached the 9th degree, making the octave unstable. The modulation to **Rast on 5** (note: on 5, *not* Upper Rast 5/8 as in Maqam Rast) is the theoretical "Maqam Basandida."

### Maqam Nikriz (family: Nikriz; printed pp.399–400)
- **Tonic:** C (Nikriz 1).
- **Scale (fig 24.45):** Nahawand 5 + Nikriz 1 — Jins Nikriz on tonic, with Nahawand on the 5th.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.46), bottom-up:** Nikriz 1 (start, tonic); Hijazkar 5; Rast 5 / Nikriz 5; Nahawand Murassaʿ 5; Hijazkar 9; Kurd 9 / Bayati 9; Upper ʿAjam 4/7; ʿAjam 7; Nahawand 5 (start-and-end, on the 5th — boxed as "Maqam Nahawand 5" region in the diagram). *Note the dashed box in fig 24.46 marks a "Maqam Nahawand 5" sub-region around ʿAjam 7 / Kurd 9 / Bayati 9 / Nahawand Murassaʿ 5.*
- **Ghammaz:** the 5th degree (G) — Nahawand 5 cluster; the upper ajnas reach the **9th degree** (Hijazkar 9, Kurd 9, Bayati 9), reflecting the archaic lack of octave equivalence.
- **SAYR:** Start on root Jins Nikriz; develop to **Nahawand on the 5th** (and Rast on 5 = "Basandida"); the ghammaz-region ajnas extend up to the 9th degree rather than resolving cleanly at the octave (archaic instability); cadence to tonic.
- **Modulations (ordered):** to Nahawand on 5; to Rast on 5 (Basandida — no complete songs, but Abdel Wahab's "il-albi yama-ntazar" uses it prominently); to Nikriz/Hijazkar on 5; up to the 9th-degree ajnas (Kurd 9, Bayati 9, Hijazkar 9).
- **Status:** rare; the authors confess they had to research and find examples. Examples: muwashshahat "ya qawam al-bani," "zarani tahta al-ghayahib," "bayna qasiyunin wa rabwa" (Omar al-Batsh); dawr "il-albi yama-ntazar" (Abdel Wahab); dawr "yalli awamak yaʿgibni" (Sayed Darwish); verses in Umm Kulthum's "baʿid ʿannak," "ansak," "ya msahharni."

### Maqam Nawa Athar (family: Nikriz; printed pp.400–401)
- **Tonic:** C (Nikriz 1). *nawa athar* = Persian *nav* (new/fresh) + *athar* (effect/trace).
- **Scale (fig 24.47):** Nikriz 1 + Hijazkar 5 — its sayr is **much more "regular"** than Nikriz's (similar to Nahawand or Suznak).
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.48), bottom-up:** Nikriz 1 (start, tonic); Hijaz 5 / Bayati 5 / Kurd 5; Nahawand 1; Nahawand 5; Nikriz 8 / Nahawand 8; Hijazkar 5 (start-and-end region).
- **SAYR:** Root Jins Nikriz → **Hijazkar on the 5th** as the characteristic resolution; develops through the 5th-degree cluster (Hijaz 5, Bayati 5, Kurd 5, Nahawand 5) up to the octave (Nikriz 8, Nahawand 8); regular, clean octave behavior (unlike Nikriz).
- **Status:** slightly more popular than Nikriz, though peripheral in tarab. Examples: Farid al-Atrash's "gamil gamal"; an instrumental section in Umm Kulthum's "fakkaruni"; muwashshah "adhkuru al-hubba"; Samaʿi Nawa Athar by Jamil ʿUways.

### Maqam Athar Kurd (family: Nikriz, or Kurd; printed pp.401–402)
- **Tonic:** C (Athar Kurd 1). A **hybrid** between Nawa Athar (raised 4th & 7th) and Kurd (lowered 2nd) — hence the name; a 20th-century development.
- **Scale (fig 24.49):** Athar Kurd 1 + Hijazkar 5.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.50), bottom-up:** Athar Kurd 1 (start, tonic); Kurd 1; Nahawand 1; Nikriz 1; Saba Zamzam 1; Saba Zamzam 5 / Kurd 5; Hijazkar 5; (top region).
- **SAYR:** Treated alternately as part of the 5-note Nikriz family or the 4-note Kurd family. Develops to Hijazkar 5 / Saba Zamzam 5 / Kurd 5; the lower region carries Kurd 1 / Nahawand 1 / Nikriz 1 / Saba Zamzam 1 modulations on the tonic.
- **Status:** very rare, never fully developed. Only complete song identified: "ya fagri nurak" (vocals Baligh Hamdi, music Yusuf Shawqi). Two Leila Mourad songs use Athar Kurd as a jins modulation within Nahawand ("il-hana wil-widd," v.3) or within Kurd ("raydak," v.3); Abdel Halim Hafez's "habibaha" v.3 modulates to Athar Kurd at "wa sirtu wahdi sharidan."

## KURD FAMILY (printed pp.401–404)

**Family overview:** Built on Jins Kurd on the tonic (conventionally D). Kurd "really only came into its own as a maqam in the 20th century" and remains fairly rare in tarab. It evolved from Maqam Hijazkar Kurd (the "Hijazkar Kurdi" muwashshahat of Min Kunuzina). By mid-20th c. Egyptian Kurd had an ascending sayr resembling Bayati/Hijaz (modulations to Nahawand and Rast on the 4th degree); it later developed experimental chromatic modulations (the "1950s Expansion"), then was simplified again for pop (losing the Rast-4 modulation). One of the two dominant pop maqamat (with Nahawand). Note the family includes Hijazkar Kurd, which is **two distinct maqamat** sharing the name (see below).

### Maqam Kurd (family: Kurd; printed pp.401–403)
- **Tonic:** D (Kurd 1).
- **Scale (fig 24.51):** Kurd 1 + Nahawand 4 — Jins Kurd on tonic, Nahawand on the 4th.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.52), bottom-up:** Kurd 1 (start, tonic); Hijaz 1; Kurd 8 (octave; rounded/start-and-end); Bayati 8; ʿAjam 6; Rast 4; Nahawand 4 (top; with arrow — see below).
- **Ghammaz:** the 4th degree (G) — Nahawand 4 / Rast 4.
- **SAYR:** Ascending, resembling Bayati and Hijaz: start on root Jins Kurd → modulate to **Nahawand on 4** and **Rast on 4** → up to ʿAjam 6 and the octave (Kurd 8, Bayati 8) → cadence to tonic. (The diagram shown represents both the mid-20th-c. Egyptian consensus and the lingering pop structure; pop Kurd loses the Rast-4 modulation.)
- **Modulations (ordered):** to Nahawand on 4; to Rast on 4 (dropped in later pop); to ʿAjam on 6; to the octave (Kurd 8, Bayati 8); to Hijaz on 1.
- **Siblings:** Hijazkar Kurd (archaic Kurd-from-octave-via-Hijazkar), Kurd 1950s Expansion (chromatic). Examples: dawr "ana haweit" (Sayed Darwish); muwashshahat "yamurru ʿujubani," "ya bahjet ir-roh"; Leila Mourad's "sanatein w-ana ahayil fik"; Karem Mahmoud's "mashghul ʿaleik"; Samaʿi Kurd by Abdo Dagher.

### Maqam Hijazkar Kurd (family: Kurd; printed p.403)
- **Tonic:** the name covers **two distinct maqamat**: (1) the archaic classification of what most now call Maqam Kurd (covered above); and (2) an archaic version of Kurd (conceptually on C, the Hijazkar tonic) whose **sayr starts with Jins Hijazkar on the octave, then lowers the "raised" intervals on the way down to a qafla on the root Jins Kurd**. Parallel to Ottoman Turkish Makam "Kürdili Hicazkar." This entry describes type (2) — fig 24.53/24.54.
- **Scale (fig 24.53):** Hijazkar 8 + Nahawand 4 + Kurd 1.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.54), bottom-up:** Kurd 1 (start-and-end, on tonic, with arrow); Nahawand 4 / Rast 4 / Nikriz 4; Kurd 8; Hijazkar 8 (top — rounded/start, the octave).
- **SAYR:** **Starts with Hijazkar on the octave**, descends, lowering the raised intervals, to a qafla on the root Jins Kurd; the 4th-degree region carries Nikriz 4 / Rast 4 / Nahawand 4.
- **Note:** Min Kunuzina files both types as "Hijazkar Kurd"; the ones actually using Hijazkar in their sayr are "tif ya durri," "ya dha al-qawam al-samhari," and the khana of "hibbi zurni ma tayassar." Early taqtuqa "izzay tibi ni-ana ashtirik" (Saleh Abdel Hayy).

### Maqam Kurd (1950s Expansion) (family: Kurd; printed p.404)
- **Tonic:** D (Kurd 1). Egyptian composers' chromatic-experimentation version (1950s on), using Western instruments/harmony; Kurd was the "perfect receptacle" (semitonal + rare in older repertoire, so free of strong associations).
- **Scale (fig 24.55):** a chromatic scale.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.56), bottom-up:** Kurd 1 (start, tonic); Hijaz 1 / Lami 1 / Saba Zamzam 1 / Athar Kurd 1 / Nahawand 1 (a tall stack of tonic-region modulations); ʿAjam 4 / Rast 4 / Bayati 5; Nahawand 4 / ʿAjam 6 (top).
- **SAYR:** Greatly expanded — a wide array of chromatic modulations, many on the tonic itself (Hijaz 1, Lami 1, Saba Zamzam 1, Athar Kurd 1, Nahawand 1) plus the 4th-degree and 5th/6th-degree ajnas.
- **Examples:** Leila Mourad's "raydak"; Umm Kulthum's "awwidti ʿeini"; Ahmad Adaweya's "ya bint is-sultan."

## ʿAJAM FAMILY (printed pp.405–407)

**Family overview:** *ʿajam* = "foreign" (Arabic), specifically Persia/Iran. The basic scale is conceptually the Western major scale — "in some sense foreign to the core of Arabic music." Despite being semitonal it has become *rarer* in the pop era (unlike Kurd/Nahawand). Two versions: the **Egyptian ʿAjam** (with a ghammaz on 5, sayr resembling Suznak/Nahawand; dating to Sayed Darwish — Shawq Afza is its main variant), and the archaic **ʿAjam ʿUshayran**, which has a *completely different tonal emphasis* (tonicizes the 3rd & 6th, like Huzam).

### Maqam ʿAjam (Egyptian version) (family: ʿAjam; printed pp.405–406)
- **Tonic:** conventionally Bb (ʿAjam 1).
- **Scale (fig 24.57):** ʿAjam 1 + Upper ʿAjam 5/8 + Nahawand 5 — Jins ʿAjam on tonic; the 5th carries Nahawand.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.58), bottom-up:** ʿAjam 1 (start, tonic); Hijazkar 1 / Hijaz 1 (low, on tonic); Saba Dalanshin 3/5; Hijaz 5; Nahawand 5; ʿAjam 8 (octave); Upper ʿAjam 5/8 (top — with arrow).
- **Ghammaz:** the 5th degree — Nahawand 5 / Hijaz 5 / Upper ʿAjam 5/8 (sayr resembles Suznak/Nahawand).
- **SAYR:** Root Jins ʿAjam → develop around the ghammaz on 5 (Nahawand 5, Hijaz 5, Upper ʿAjam 5/8), with Saba Dalanshin 3/5 available → octave (ʿAjam 8) → cadence to tonic.
- **Modulations (ordered):** to Nahawand on 5; to Upper ʿAjam on 5/8; to Hijaz on 5; to Saba Dalanshin on 3/5; to the octave ʿAjam 8; to Hijaz/Hijazkar on 1.
- **Siblings:** Shawq Afza (Hijaz on 5 prominent — the main variant), ʿAjam ʿUshayran (3rd/6th tonicization). Examples: Sayed Darwish's "tilʿit ya m-ahla nurha"; Iraqi "talʿa min beit abuha" (Nazem al-Ghazali); Umm Kulthum's "aghadan alqak"; Warda's "fi youm wi leila"; Muhammad Qandil's "habl il-widad inʿataʾ."

### Maqam Shawq Afza (family: ʿAjam; printed p.406)
- **Tonic:** Bb (ʿAjam 1). *shawq afza* = "more longing/yearning" (Persian).
- **Scale (fig 24.59):** ʿAjam 1 + Hijaz 5 — the **prominence of Jins Hijaz on 5** is the distinguishing characteristic, plus Saba Dalanshin 3/5 (mislabeled "Saba 3" by many).
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.60), bottom-up:** ʿAjam 1 (start, tonic); Bayati 5; Nahawand 5; Upper ʿAjam 5/8; Saba Dalanshin 3/5; Hijaz 5 (start-and-end, on the ghammaz — defining).
- **SAYR:** Root Jins ʿAjam → **Hijaz on the 5th** as the characteristic emphasis (the ʿAjam analogue of Suznak), with Saba Dalanshin 3/5, Bayati 5, Nahawand 5, Upper ʿAjam 5/8 available.
- **Status:** the most common variant of Egyptian ʿAjam. Examples: Umm Kulthum's "lissa fakir"; Fairouz's "zahrit il-janub (iswarit il-ʿarus)"; Longa ʿAjam by Abdo Dagher.

### Maqam ʿAjam ʿUshayran (family: ʿAjam; printed pp.406–407)
- **Tonic:** conventionally F (ʿAjam ʿUshayran). NOT simply an archaic ʿAjam with a different sayr — it has a **completely different tonal emphasis**: instead of tonicizing the 5th (as Rast, Nahawand, Nikriz, and Egyptian ʿAjam do), it **tonicizes the 3rd and 6th degrees** (as Huzam does, but very differently). The authors confess this is a rare maqam they had to research.
- **Scale (fig 24.61):** Nahawand 6 + ʿAjam 1 + Kurd 3 — note the strong Nahawand on 6 and Kurd on 3.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.62), bottom-up:** ʿAjam 1 (start, tonic); Nahawand 6 LO; Kurd 3 / Bayati 3; Nahawand 5; Upper ʿAjam 5/8; Saba Dalanshin 6/8; Bayati 10 / Hijazkar 10; Nahawand 9; Kurd 10; Nahawand 6; ʿAjam 8 (top, octave — rounded/end region).
- **Emphasis:** the **3rd degree** (Bayati 3, Kurd 3, and Hijazkar on 3) and the **6th degree** (Nahawand 6) — plus a strong Nahawand on 6. These ajnas make it very distinct from Egyptian ʿAjam, though a few overlap (e.g. descending Nahawand 5).
- **SAYR:** Develops via the 3rd-degree ajnas (Bayati/Kurd on 3, Hijazkar on 3) and the 6th-degree Nahawand, with the octave region (ʿAjam 8) reached at the top; tonicization on 3 and 6 rather than 5.
- **Status:** archaic as an independent maqam (rarely-performed muwashshahat like "daʿi al-hawa qad saha," "ayqadha al-hubbu fuʾadi," "ma li ʿayni absarat"); exists in very early Egyptian recordings (taqtuqa "il-hubbi fi albi amana," Fathiyya Ahmad). More common as a **modulation on the 6th scale degree of Maqam Bayati or Maqam Saba** (e.g. Umm Kulthum's "huwwa sahih" v.3 at "il-albi ah").

## OTHER MAQAMAT (single maqamat listed alongside the families)

### Maqam Saba (printed pp.407–409)
*(Listed alongside the families despite being a single maqam, due to its prominence; the prompt flags it as high-priority.)*
- **Tonic:** conventionally D (Saba 1). Root jins identical to Bayati but with a **lowered 4th scale degree**; features a prominent Jins ʿAjam on 6. *saba* = "East Wind" (Arabic).
- **Scale (fig 24.63):** Saba 1 + Hijaz 3 + ʿAjam 6 + Nikriz 6 — unique interval sequence between degrees 2, 3, 4, 5.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.64), bottom-up:** Saba 1 (start, tonic); Rast 4 LO; Bayati 1 (on tonic); ʿAjam 3 / Jiharkah 3; Nahawand 4 / Rast 4; Upper ʿAjam 3/6; Nikriz 6; Saba 8 (flat octave — see below); Hijaz 3 (rounded/start-and-end); ʿAjam 6 (rounded/start-and-end, on the 6th — defining).
- **Ghammaz / emphasis:** the **3rd degree** (Hijaz on 3) and the **6th degree** (ʿAjam on 6) are the primary modulation points; occasionally the octave; also Jins Rast underneath the tonic (Rast 4 LO), similar to Bayati and Hijaz.
- **SAYR:** Highly **constrained** — Saba doesn't branch and has few options from each of its few modulation points (mostly 3 and 6). Start on root Jins Saba → the signature moves are **Hijaz on 3** and **ʿAjam on 6**; occasionally the octave tonic or Jins Rast below the tonic; the sayr "tends to repeat a small set of common pathways," adding to the maqam's intensity. **Strongest remaining maqam that lacks octave equivalence** — its 8th degree isn't even an octave above the tonic; it's a *flat octave* ("Saba 8"). Many folk songs stay in the lower jins and avoid the octave altogether.
- **Modulations (ordered):** to Hijaz on 3; to ʿAjam on 6; to the flat octave (Saba 8) [occasional]; to Rast below the tonic (Rast 4 LO); to ʿAjam/Jiharkah on 3; to Nahawand/Rast on 4; to Nikriz on 6; to Upper ʿAjam 3/6.
- **Mood:** "almost universally agreed upon as being melancholy."
- **Examples:** qasida "uhibbuki ya salma" (Yusuf al-Manyalawi); dawr "addi m-ahibbak zaʿlan minnak" (Saleh Abdel Hayy); muwashshahat "ahwa qamaran," "ghuddi jufunik"; "baʿrit haha," "ya falastiniyya" (Sheikh Imam); Aleppan qudud "skaba ya dumuʿ il-ʿein," "halla halla ya jamlo," "doumik doum," "ya m-asʿad es-sabahiyya"; Umm Kulthum's "huwwa sahih"; Ahmad Adaweya's "salamitha ummu hasan," "habba fouʾ"; Hakim's "wala wahid (wala milyun)."

### Maqam Saba Zamzam (printed pp.409–410)
- **Tonic:** D (Saba Zamzam 1) — a **semitonal version of Saba** (with a flat 2nd scale degree).
- **Scale (fig 24.65):** Saba Zamzam 1 + Hijaz 3 + ʿAjam 6 + Nikriz 6 (same upper structure as Saba).
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.66), bottom-up:** Saba Zamzam 1 (start, tonic); Athar Kurd 1 / Kurd 1 (on tonic — its most frequent use); Upper ʿAjam 3/6; Hijaz 3; Nikriz 6; ʿAjam 6 (top).
- **SAYR:** Similar to Saba (Hijaz 3, ʿAjam 6, Nikriz 6, Upper ʿAjam 3/6), **but** its distinctive and most frequent modulations are **to Kurd and Athar Kurd on the root tonic** — "where it was really used most frequently."
- **Status:** mostly a jins modulation in theory, but found several viable pathways with the mid-20th-c. expansion of Kurd and Athar Kurd. Examples: Ahmad Adaweya's "ya bint is-sultan"; endings of Abdel Halim Hafez's "sawwah" and "ala hisbi widad galbi"; intro of Umm Kulthum's "amal hayati" (modulates to Saba Zamzam).

### Maqam Lami (printed pp.410–411)
- **Tonic:** the Iraqi maqam, set *outside* the Kurd family. Basically **Kurd with a flat 5th scale degree** (Iraqi intonation differs somewhat). Created by Muhammad al-Qubbanji (early 20th c.).
- **Scale (fig 24.67):** Lami 1 + Kurd 4 (the Jins Lami bracket spans 5 notes because the melodic activity of Jins Lami includes the 5th degree, the note above the ghammaz).
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.68) — very small:** Lami 1 (start, tonic); Kurd 4 / Nahawand 4 / Nikriz 4. That's the whole diagram.
- **SAYR:** Minimal; modulations to Kurd 4 / Nahawand 4 / Nikriz 4 on the 4th degree.
- **Status:** experimental novelty; set outside the Kurd family because Kurd-family maqamat never modulate *to* it. Abdel Wahab pioneered it in the sharqi repertoire ("il-burtuʿal," then "albi bi ulli kalam"); Abdel Mottaleb's "tislam idein illi-shtara"; Wadih al-Safi's "in-najmat saru yisʿalu"; Sabri al-Mudallal's "Ahmad ya habibi."

### Maqam Jiharkah (printed p.411)
- **Tonic:** conventionally F (Jiharkah 1) — resembles ʿAjam but with distinctive intonation: the **3rd degree somewhere between Rast's and ʿAjam's**, and a **slightly lowered 4th degree**.
- **Scale (fig 24.69):** Jiharkah 1 + Upper Rast 5/8 — with a downward arrow on the 4th (reflecting the lowered, unstable 4th).
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.70), bottom-up:** Jiharkah 1 (start, tonic — rounded/start); Sikah Baladi 1; Bayati 6 LO / Upper Rast 5/8 LO (below tonic); Nahawand 2; ʿAjam Murassaʿ 1; Upper Rast 5/8; Nahawand 5; Hijaz 5.
- **SAYR:** Root Jins Jiharkah → up through the ghammaz region (Upper Rast 5/8, Nahawand 5, Hijaz 5), with lower-region modulations (Bayati 6 LO, Upper Rast 5/8 LO) and ʿAjam Murassaʿ 1 / Nahawand 2 / Sikah Baladi 1.
- **Status:** used to be much more common than ʿAjam, but archaic by mid-20th c. (no new songs composed in it). Kept alive by musicians who retain the jins as a modulation within Rast. Examples: Farid al-Atrash's "fouʾ ghusnik ya lamuna"; muwashshahat "anta sultanu al-milah," "basimun ʿan laʾal," "kallili ya suhbu"; Aleppan qudud "ar-rozana," "wahyat ʿeinayya."

### Maqam Sikah Baladi (printed pp.411–412)
- **Tonic:** Sikah-region — "arguably the most challenging maqam," a specialty of virtuosos. Its scale/sayr is a **hybrid between a transposition of Huzam and an ordinary non-Sikah note** — the intervals match neither just nor equal-tempered intonation, "impossible to reproduce on anything but the voice and fretless instruments."
- **Scale (fig 24.71):** Sikah Baladi 8 + (Pseudo-Hijazkar/suspended 5th) + Sikah Baladi 1 — note the "Pseudo-Hijazkar/suspended 5th" bracket below.
- **Ajnas (sayr fig 24.72), bottom-up:** Sikah Baladi 1 (start, tonic); Nahawand 3 / Bayati 3 / Hijaz 3; Pseudo-Hijaz 5; Jiharkah 8 / Nahawand 8 / Hijazkar 8; Nahawand 10 / Hijaz 10 / Bayati 10; Sikah Baladi 8 (top — rounded/start-and-end, octave).
- **SAYR:** Start on the root Sikah Baladi → 3rd-degree ajnas (Hijaz 3, Bayati 3, Nahawand 3) and a **Pseudo-Hijaz / suspended 5th**; ascends to the octave region (Hijazkar 8, Nahawand 8, Jiharkah 8) and high ajnas on degree 10; the octave (Sikah Baladi 8) is itself a start/cadence region. The unique non-tempered intervals make it sound "a bit like both" Huzam and a non-Sikah maqam.
- **Status:** a favored display of compositional virtuosity (esp. Abdel Wahab after he "gave up Saba Dalanshin in everything"). Examples: Abdel Wahab's "sahirtu minhu al-layali," "il-habib il-maghul," "ahwak" (for Abdel Halim Hafez); Nagat's "ma-staghmash" v.2 (Kamal al-Tawil); "aruh li min" v.3; "shams il-asil" v.3 (both Riyad al-Sunbati for Umm Kulthum).
